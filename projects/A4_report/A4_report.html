Proposition: Under Mayor Bill de Blasio, the police became less abusive of their power

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Filtering out Unsubstantiated Reports
    • Rating: -1.5
    • The Unsubstantiated reports, when added to the visualization, do not support the story that I'm trying to tell, which is critical of deBlasio. While it is not untrue that the number of exonerated and substantiated reports has increased under deBlasio, this hides the fact that total number of complaints has actually decreased under deBlasio.
  • Filtering out 2019 Data
    • Rating: -2
    • The 2019 data shows the total number of complaints decreasing, which is not consistent with the story that I'm trying to show. By filtering out the 2019 data, it makes the increase in complaints more salient, which is emphasized by the arrow. There is no reason not to include this data besides wanting to make the chosen story more persuasive.
  • Make the total reports arrow grow thicker over time
    • Rating: 1
    • This is an earnest and subtle way to emphasize the change in number of reports over time. It draws the reader's attention to the totel number of reports increasing and the arrow suggests that a future increase is imminent. Because the rest of the data filtering is so deceptive, this design decision serves to support it, but on its own it's a nice touch.
  • Annotate 2014 and color-code republican and democratic mayoral times
    • Rating: 2
    • The point of the visualization is to show the effect of deBlasio's tenure on police complaint reports. Bloomberg, a Republican, was mayor from 2002-2013, and deBlasio, a Democrat, was mayor from 2014-2019, so annotating 2014 and color-coding the mayoral times is a good way to emphasize the change in leadership. The blue and red colors are classes democrat/republican colors, and I hand drew an annotation to make the year that deBlasio took office more perceptually obvious.
  • Many tick marks and grid lines
    • Rating: 1
    • This design choice is subtle, but it's always useful to have gridlines to more easily compare values. The deception comes in when you take into consideration the other deceptive factors in the chart, which this feature continues to support.

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Including all years and all types of reports
    • Rating: 2
    • Luckily, including all of the data supports the conclusion that under deBlasio, complaints against police officers have decreased. Especially compared against the prior visualization, this is a very honest way to present the data.
  • Order of vertical bar stacks
    • Rating: -1
    • Although all of the data is there, the order of the vertical bar graphs is intentional, and it emphasizes the decrease in unsubstantiated complaints while masking the increase in exonerated complaints by placing each bar on an unaligned axis. Any other order would make this increase more obvious, so I didn't want to highlight this. It doesn't show the whole story.
  • No tick marks or grid lines
    • Rating: -0.5
    • In contrast to the visualization above, I did not include grid lines in the graph because the first few bars after deBlasio took office don't actually easily support the story that the graph is trying to tell, and I wanted to dissuade the reader form looking too hard at those initial years and comparing values to each other. Instead, I wanted them to focus on the more abvious decrease right at the end.
  • Annotate 2014 and color-code republican and democratic mayoral times
    • Rating: 1.5
    • With similar reasoning as in the first visualization, I annotated the background with blue and red to emphasize republican and deBlasio's tenure, and I still think this is earnest. The bar colors of the earlier years blend into the backgroung slightly, which is intentional, as it serves as a good contrast for the "clarity" that deBlasio's democratic tenure brings (which is why this rating isn't a full 2).

Final Reflection

I wanted to challenge myself to use very similar visualizations for both of my graphs in order to reach oppose conclusions, and I was surprised to see how easy it was to do so. When I approached the data with a particular agenda, I found it rather easy to limit the reader's information or use common visual markers that we've discussed in class to make the graphs more persuasive. I even manipulated the order of visualization on this web page to make you see the more manipulated graph with the missing data before seeing the graph with the more complete information - if I did not create a second visualization, it would be easy to simply present the first one as ground truth. You don't always know what you don't know, and not attributing where data comes from or how categories are defined makes it easier to mislead.

Even more "honest" visualizations can use deceptive tactics. I thought my second visualization made more earnest choices regarding data inclusion, and I think it represents the data more accurately (that the total numer of complaints dropped, but the proportion of complaints that were exonerated or labelled unsubstantiated), but in my intention to present a strong trend in the data, I made choices that masked the uncertainties in the data and tried to present the trend as stronger than it actually is. The real world is complicated, and if you have an agenda, it's easy to overstate the certainty of your conclusions, even if you're not outright lying.

While there were several tactics that I thought on their own were not deceptive, but could be used to further deceptive claims, I discovered one that made me realize where I draw my boundary between "acceptable" persuasive design choices versus "misleading" ones: Excluding data from analysis should always cause hesitation. There are legitimate reasons to exclude data (irrelevance, data corruption, etc), but outliers and trends that don't support your initial assumptions are perfect targets as sources of deception, and they should be treated with care and due diligence.
